Presentation Abstracts

Session 1

Panagiotis Gkikopoulos (UniNE)
Data-Centric Consensus in Smart Cities

Smart cities operate large networks of devices and sensors. They produce large amounts of data critical for the reliable operation of services. Such data-driven systems are susceptible to data quality issues, especially when AI models are involved. Data-Centric Consensus is a proposed combination of multi-view data fusion and data cleansing to improve data quality by filling gaps and resolving conflicts. We present and evaluate the DCC methodology, focusing on conflict resolution using voting. We then showcase 2 application fields, indoor localisation in smart shopping and people flow tracking in public transport.

Peterson Yuhala (UniNE)
Securing IoT Peripherals with Trusted Execution Environments and Machine Learning

With the increasing popularity of Internet of Things (IoT) devices, security concerns have become a major challenge: confidential information, e.g., speech, images, etc. is constantly being transmitted (sometimes inadvertently) from user devices to untrusted cloud services. We propose a design to enhance security and privacy in IoT based systems by isolating hardware peripheral drivers in a trusted execution environment (TEE), and leveraging secure machine learning classification techniques to filter out sensitive data (i.e., speech, images, etc.) from the associated peripheral device before it makes its way to an untrusted party in the cloud. We provide a proof-of-concept implementation of our security design with secure sound processing via I2S.

Rémi Dulong and Nathan Felber(UniNE)
RDMA enhanced by In-Network features

Remote Direct Memory Access (RDMA) protocols, designed to provide fast transfers and low latencies over entire clusters have been in use for two decades. The main ones (InfiniBand and iWarp) evolved and followed the constant evolution of networks over the years, through compatible hardware such as network interfaces (NICs) and switches. Nowadays, this hardware can reach 100Gbit/s, or even 400Gbit/s. However, the feature set of RDMA remains limited. Implementing some advanced features in a pure software fashion can severely reduce performance, if the said feature does not comply with basic RDMA operations workflow. That is why we try to use a programmable switch (Intel Tofino) to implement these features in the network itself.

Session 2

Jämes Ménétrey (UniNE)
A Cross-platform Pub/Sub system with Attestation

We explore the integration of attestation mechanisms into pub/sub systems to enhance security, transparency, and the verifiability of message exchanges within distributed computing environments.

Vincenzo Di Perna (UniNE)
Enhancing Blockchain System Evaluation through Extended DiabloBench Support for Kollapse: A Case Study on Expiring Money and Economic Circulation

Blockchain technology has ushered in a new era for distributed systems by offering decentralization and security. However, assessing the performance and functionalities of diverse blockchain systems presents a formidable challenge, owing to the resource-intensive nature of benchmarking and the scarcity of comprehensive literature. The need for substantial hardware and economic resources for result replication often surpasses practical feasibility, leading to constrained evaluations. Moreover, the absence of functional systems and repositories compounds these limitations. This presentation tackles the intricate problem of resource-intensive evaluations within the context of blockchain systems benchmarking. We introduce an inventive strategy that involves the expansion of the DiabloBench benchmarking framework to natively incorporate support for Kollapse, an experimental environment tailored for testing distributed systems. Focusing on the domain of programmable money, which empowers the implementation of customized code and parameters to define behavioral attributes, we delve into the evaluation of blockchain systems. Specifically, we scrutinize the incorporation of an expiration mechanism, wherein time constraints are associated with monetary units. Our analysis seeks to determine whether this approach stimulates economic circulation and incentivizes the judicious utilization of financial resources. Our endeavor not only streamlines benchmarking procedures but also facilitates equitable comparisons across different blockchain systems. In effect, this presentation advocates for the progression of distributed systems by addressing the challenge of costly evaluation. Through our proposed methodology, we contribute to the advancement of blockchain technology and its potential impact on economic mechanisms.

Alisson Medeiros (UniBE)
Flow Latency and Throughput Aware Sensitive Routing for 6DoF VR

The next generation of Virtual Reality (VR) applications is expected to provide advanced experiences through Degree-of-Freedom (DoF) content, which requires latency and throughput guarantees. This research presents a novel method for optimizing the latency performance for DoF VR applications by calculating paths based on their latency requirement. The proposed approach (FLATWISE) balances the shortest and widest paths to minimize the overall latency performance for all DoF VR applications. We investigate the Joint Flow Allocation (JFA) problem to find paths for all flows in a network such that it determines the optimal paths for each flow in terms of throughput and latency. FLATWISE allocates paths for flows with latency close to the latency required by flow while satisfying the flow throughput requirement. To evaluate the effectiveness of FLATWISE, extensive simulations were con- ducted and compared against state-of-the-art routing algorithms. The results demonstrate that FLATWISE significantly improves flow latency reduction, over-provisioned latency reduction, end- to-end (E2E) latency reduction, flow throughput enhancement, frame rate enhancement, and algorithm execution time reduction.

Dimitrios Xenakis (UniBE)
Iterative Inlier Selection (IIS) for Flip-Ambiguity Mitigation in Cooperative Localization

Enhancing the accuracy of positioning estimations in wireless sensor networks remains an active area of research. One effective strategy is enabling sensors to collaborate during their positioning, known as Cooperative Localization (CL). In our prior work, we introduced the ARLCL framework, which offers superior performance when compared to the alternative approaches. However, a consistent challenge across CL techniques is the flip-ambiguity problem which manifests when the positioning of individual node-neighborhoods is accurate, yet the relations between different neighborhoods are incorrect because of their relative misplacement and wrong orientation. To address this, we propose a correcting method based on Iterative Inlier Selection (IIS) that is designed to mitigate such errors. Evaluating the efficacy of IIS, we undertook a real-world deployment experiment spanning a floor of 500m2, using 40 UWB nodes. Our results showed that IIS improved the performance of all assessed CL methods by up to 50%. Furthermore, the utility of IIS extends beyond CL and is relevant for any scientific domain confronted with inlier/outlier selection.

Session 3

Zimu Xu (UniBE)
Predicting Personality Traits from Device Usage via Semi-supervised Federated Learning

In the era of ubiquitous computing, smart devices are deeply ingrained in our daily routines. Their extensive interaction logs can provide profound insights into user behavior and, potentially, their personality traits. A potential forthcoming study is poised to leverage device usage data for personality prediction, with a focus on privacy-preservation through Federated Learning (FL) techniques. Given the challenge of obtaining sufficient labeled data through personality questionnaires, I introduce a semi-supervised approach within the federated learning paradigm. This method allows for utilizing both labeled (from the limited questionnaires) and unlabeled data (large-scale device usage logs) in a decentralized manner, offering a promising solution to the problem of incomplete labeling. This approach ensures that raw user data never leaves the device, addressing potential privacy concerns.

Jesutofunmi Ajayi (UniBE)
Learning Competitive Algorithms for Network Slice Provisioning

The capabilities of 5G Mobile Networks are expected to be bolstered by the virtualization of the network infrastructure, as well as the integration of edge-based computing systems into the telco environment. As a result, Network Slicing has recently emerged as a novel virtualized infrastructure model in which heterogeneous services can be simultaneously provisioned over a common mobile network infrastructure. The provisioning of such services in the 5G network, however, is preceded by their admission which is a critical decision-making mechanism during slice preparation since it determines whether or not upcoming network slice requests can be admitted into the system. In this presentation, we explore this problem and look at how Bandit Learning can be leveraged to adaptively select admission control policies, in the form of competitive algorithms, towards the admission and provisioning of Network Slices in 5G mobile networks.

Eric Samikwa (UniBE)
Dynamic Federated Learning: Navigating Data and Resource Heterogeneity in Edge IoT Environments

Federated Learning (FL) in edge Internet of Things (IoT) environments is challenging due to the heterogeneous nature of the learning environment, primarily embodied in two aspects. Firstly, the data gathered from geographically dispersed clients often exhibits statistical heterogeneity, usually non-independent and identically distributed, which can deteriorate the FL training accuracy. Secondly, the heterogeneous computing and communication resources available across IoT devices introduce instability in training processes. This instability results in prolonged global model convergence times and compromises energy efficiency. While existing solutions tend to address only one facet of this heterogeneity issue, they overlook the conjoined challenge of resource and data heterogeneity, particularly concerning IoT systems with constrained resources. We introduce Dynamic Federated Learning (DFL) as a solution to confront the dual problem of data and resource heterogeneity for distributed training in edge IoT. DFL operates through a fusion of resource-aware split computing for deep neural networks and a dynamic clustering mechanism. The latter clusters training participants based on the similarity of their sub-model layers, fostering collaboration. Results demonstrate the efficacy of DFL in enhancing key training parameters, including training time, accuracy, and energy consumption, in heterogeneous learning landscapes characterized by data and resource heterogeneity.

Session 4

Ana Leni Frei (UniBE)
Geometric deep learning for epithelial cell classification from H&E images in rectal cancer and tumor microenvironment analysis

Cell-level tissue analysis are often used in digital histopathology to study tissue structure and composition. Nowadays, available datasets and models for automatic cell segmentation and classification exist but lack the differentiation of epithelial cells into normal and malignant. This distinction can be challenging due to the high morphologic heterogeneity of malignant cells. To tackle this, we developed an epithelial cell-based graph node classification model to leverage both local cell features and overall tissue architecture to accurately classify epithelial cell. The proposed method showed excellent F1 score performance on two test sets and performed significantly better than conventional computer vision methods (p < 0.05). This highlights that structural context, captured by graphs, is an important feature for epithelial cell classification. Furthermore, the proposed model can be applied on top of any other method that identifies epithelial cells to further differentiate between normal and malignant cells and gain more insight about tissue composition. Based on this, fully automated cell-level analysis can be applied on biopsies and resections of rectal cancer patients who have undergone neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapies to study tumor microenvironenment, tumor characteristics and study treatment response.

Pasquale De Rosa (UniNE)
Using Machine Learning Techniques for Accurate Link Predictions on Large-Scale Dynamic Graphs

Graphs are structures made of vertices (nodes) and edges (links). Most real-world graphs are dynamic, i.e. their structure progressively change over time. A dynamic graph can also be viewed as a time series, where additional links continuously form between new or existing nodes over a specific period of time. My study is focusing on the analysis of ML-based algorithms to forecast the generation of new links on such dynamic graphs. My aim is to propose a new link prediction algorithm capable of outperforming the state-of-the-art approaches (i.e. GCNs, GraphSAGE, HinSAGE) in this specific task. This new algorithm will further be used in real-world applications, such as predicting the generation of new links on dynamic blockchain graphs.

Session 5

Chuyang Gao (UniBE)
Reinforcement Learning based Joint Optimization for Edge Caching in Mobile immersive communication.

Edge caching in immersive communication is an effective solution for reducing traffic congestion and minimizing access delay to popular content. However, finding the optimal cache decision policy poses challenges. This paper proposes an approach to address these challenges by offering three key contributions. Firstly, it reduces computational complexity, enabling real-time decision making by combining optimization techniques with data-driven AI algorithms. Secondly, our approach supports partial caching, enabling more effective utilization of cached content among multiple users and enhancing multi-user video caching. Thirdly, it directly predicts the optimal caching strategy, eliminating the need for content popularity comparisons and considering network bandwidth and storage capacity constraints for content replacement. Experimental validation demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach in terms of computational efficiency, reducing latency and improved caching efficiency through partial caching.

Simon Queyrut (UniNE)
KillerDroid-robust Malware Detectors

The Android operating system's remarkably widespread adoption has brought about a concerning proliferation of malware within the Android ecosystem. This surge in malicious software propagation presents a serious challenge, underscoring the need to effectively detect such threats. Still, the reported outcomes in the field of malware static analysis, along with existing solutions, have shown a tendency to greatly overestimate their actual efficacy. In a recent study, a novel methodology for generating a very large amount of realistic, carefully crafted edge cases of Android malwares is presented. The obfuscation suite manages to elude cutting-edge scanners both within academic and industry domains. We explain the inefficiency of the current generation of antiviruses in the face of the new adversarial software generator and set the groundwork for an efficient malware detector through learning and rule-based methods.

Mingjing Sun (UniBE)
Networking for Immersive Communications: Floating Service

The vision of immersive communication is to enable interactions with remote people and environments such that users get the impression to be really inside a remote or virtual environment. Augmented Reality (AR) and Virtual Reality (VR) are key technologies to enable immersive communications. However, immersive applications supported by multiple edge computing devices (MEC) becomes complicated when service users become highly mobile, e.g. in the case of users moving in vehicles such as cars and trains. In this way, services have to be migrated among edge servers. We aim to consider the development of the Floating Services (FS) concept to address the problem of finding and deploying services.

Session 6

Sébastien Rumley (HES)
Energy efficiency of internet traffic - models and bound

The Information and Communications Technologies (ICT) sector is increasingly scrutinized and indicted for its role in carbon emissions, which could amount to those of the aviation sector. In this context, it is crucial for the ICT community (industry, academia, users…) to develop a solid understanding of their energy consumptions and CO2 footprint. In my talk, I will consider the energy consumption of internet traffic, and I will suggest how to approximate a lower bound for it. In the first part of the talk, I will focus on network transport. I review optical WDM links architectures and perform a breakdown of the power consumption per component. In particular, I will show that achieving a link consuming less than 1pJ/bit is challenging, and that over long distances, the consumption can be expected to be superior to 100pJ/bit/1000km. I will show how physical limits can be used to validate these lower bounds. In the second part of the talk, I will consider the consumption of network routing. Using examples taken from High Performance Computing, I will propose an approach to approximate the lower bound for routing. I will then hypothesize how far we are from these lower bounds in the wild, and finally conclude on where, in my opinion, research efforts should be directed to reduce overall internet consumption.
